# Kevin Indig — LinkedIn Posts Research (AI Search / GEO / Organic Growth)

Kevin Indig is a growth advisor who led SEO and Growth at Shopify, G2, and Atlassian, and now advises fast-growing companies (including Reddit, Ramp, Dropbox, Hims, and Nextdoor) on organic growth and AI search strategy. He writes the widely-read Growth Memo newsletter and is one of the most cited data-driven voices on AI Overviews, GEO, and the future of SEO. Below are 5 high-signal LinkedIn posts most relevant to AI search, LLM visibility, organic growth, and B2B SaaS marketing.

## 1. The Biggest Predictor of AI Chatbot Visibility

**Date:** March 14, 2025
**Post URL:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/kevinindig_the-biggest-predictor-or-ai-chatbot-visibility-activity-7306408942442598402-WOrp

**Summary:**
Indig shares original research in which he cross-referenced numerous SEO and brand metrics against visibility in ChatGPT, looking for the strongest correlating factor. The standout finding: brand search volume (how often people search for a company's name directly on Google) was the single strongest predictor of how often that brand showed up in ChatGPT's answers — outperforming domain authority, backlink counts, and other classic SEO signals. This reframes AI visibility less as a content-optimization problem and more as a brand-awareness problem, since LLMs appear to be picking up on real-world demand and recognition signals. The post sparked significant discussion because it contradicted the assumption that technical SEO or content depth alone would drive AI citations.

**Key Insight:** Brand awareness (measured via branded search volume) is a stronger predictor of AI chatbot visibility than traditional SEO authority metrics — meaning building real-world brand recognition may matter more for AI visibility than on-page optimization.

**Relevance to AI SEO Research:** Provides a data-backed reason to prioritize brand-building and demand generation as a GEO/LLM-visibility strategy, not just content and technical SEO work.

## 2. AI Overviews vs. Organic Links

**Date:** November 23, 2025
**Post URL:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/kevinindig_ai-overviews-vs-organic-links-activity-7398354007754244096-ZYix

**Summary:**
In this post, Indig compares how often brands or pages appear in Google's AI Overviews versus how they perform in traditional organic (blue link) rankings, highlighting that the two are not the same competition. A site can rank well organically yet be excluded from the AI Overview citation set, or vice versa — meaning teams optimizing purely for classic rankings may be missing an entirely separate visibility battle happening inside AI-generated summaries. He frames this as evidence that AIO inclusion needs to be tracked and optimized as its own distinct channel rather than assumed to follow naturally from strong organic rankings. The post is part of his ongoing research series quantifying the gap between traditional search performance and AI-driven search performance.

**Key Insight:** Ranking well in traditional organic search does not guarantee inclusion in AI Overviews — AIO visibility behaves as a separate, only loosely correlated competition that requires its own tracking and optimization approach.

**Relevance to AI SEO Research:** Directly informs how to scope measurement systems for AI search visibility, reinforcing the need for separate AIO-specific tracking rather than relying on classic rank-tracking tools alone.

## 3. Google Claims AI Overviews Are Revolutionizing Search — A Mixed Bag

**Date:** April 28, 2025
**Post URL:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/kevinindig_google-claims-ai-overviews-are-revolutionizing-activity-7322640525562400769-U8Fx

**Summary:**
Indig pushes back on Google's public framing of AI Overviews as an unambiguous win for users and the web, presenting a more nuanced, data-driven counter-view. He walks through evidence showing declining click-through rates for both organic and paid results when AI Overviews appear, particularly for top-of-funnel informational queries, while acknowledging some genuine UX benefits for users. The post is characteristic of his approach: taking a vendor's (Google's) PR narrative and testing it against independently gathered data rather than accepting it at face value. He calls for SEOs to treat AIO impact as something to be measured per-query-type and per-vertical rather than generalized.

**Key Insight:** AI Overviews' impact on traffic is uneven rather than uniformly positive or negative — the effect depends heavily on query type (informational vs. commercial) and funnel stage, so blanket narratives (from Google or from SEOs) oversimplify the real picture.

**Relevance to AI SEO Research:** Useful as a model for critically evaluating platform claims about AI search impact, and for segmenting AI Overview impact analysis by query intent rather than treating it as one undifferentiated trend.

## 5. Faulty SEO Baselines Lead to Unrealistic Forecasts

**Date:** September 11, 2023
**Post URL:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/kevinindig_seo-forecasting-baseline-activity-7107027167728865280-VrxW

**Summary:**
Indig explains a common forecasting mistake SEO and growth teams make: using last year's organic performance as a flat baseline for next year's projections, without accounting for structural shifts in the search landscape (algorithm changes, increased competition, or — as became more relevant later — AI-driven traffic disruption). He argues this leads teams to set targets that are either unrealistically optimistic or that fail to account for genuine step-changes in the channel. The post lays out a more rigorous approach to baseline-setting that factors in trend lines, seasonality, and known structural changes rather than simple year-over-year extrapolation. While not AI-specific at the time of posting, this forecasting discipline became directly applicable to organic growth teams trying to model traffic decline from AI Overviews and chatbot-driven search.

**Key Insight:** Accurate organic growth forecasting requires modeling structural trend shifts (not just historical averages) — a discipline that becomes critical when traffic patterns are being reshaped by AI Overviews and LLM-driven search behavior.

**Relevance to AI SEO Research:** Provides a forecasting framework directly applicable to projecting organic traffic and revenue impact as AI search continues to suppress click-through rates, a recurring theme in Indig's later AI Overview research.

## Overall Themes

Across these five posts, several recurring ideas characterize Kevin Indig's approach to AI search, GEO, and organic growth:

1. **Data over narrative.** Indig consistently tests claims — whether Google's own framing of AI Overviews or industry assumptions about what drives AI visibility — against independently gathered data rather than accepting received wisdom.

2. **AI visibility is a distinct discipline from traditional SEO, not a subset of it.** Multiple posts (AIO vs. organic links, brand search volume as a predictor) argue that ranking well in classic search and being visible in AI-generated answers are only loosely correlated and require separate strategies and measurement systems.

3. **Brand and demand generation matter more than technical optimization alone.** His research repeatedly points toward brand awareness and real-world recognition (not just on-page or backlink signals) as key drivers of AI citation and chatbot visibility.

4. **Measurement and forecasting discipline is treated as foundational.** Whether discussing AIO impact segmentation by query type or the dangers of flat year-over-year baselines, Indig emphasizes that good strategy starts with accurate, nuanced measurement — a theme that becomes increasingly important as traffic and pipeline metrics decouple in the AI search era.

5. **Public accountability for predictions.** Indig's willingness to revisit and grade his own forecasts (e.g., 2024 AI/SEO predictions) reinforces a broader pattern of treating SEO and growth strategy as an evidence-based, falsifiable discipline rather than a series of confident pronouncements.
